Norwegian Wood

Haruki Murakami


Spoilers ahead for those who care.


This was the second book of Murakami's that I've read, the first being Kafka on the Shore. I first read Kafka 3 years ago, and I immediately fell in love with it. Murakami writes in a introspective yet down-to-earth manner that I find to be profoundly beautiful. What I found the most captivating about that book was Murakami's ability to touch on many deeply personal topics with such delicacy and realism. You feel as though Murakami is a wise old man, who is guiding you, the reader, through his characters lives, showing you their mistakes and misery, while reassuring you everything will be alright in the end, as if giving you a lesson on life inspired by his own trials and tribulations.


Kafka on the Shore is generally regarded as Murakami's second or third most popular book, trading places perhaps with either 1Q84 or The Wind-Up Bird Chronicle. His most popular book, however, is without a doubt Norwegian Wood. It is often credited as the book which made Murakami famous. This is despite the fact that, among fans and even Murakami himself, Norwegian Wood is considered to be one of his weaker books. And while Norwegian Wood is far from being a bad book, it does have its flaws as compared to Kafka. That being said, Norwegian Wood is still unmistakably Murakami's, and is just as deep and insightful as Kafka.



Norwegian Wood is characterized by its rather simple plot. Murakami has stated that this book started as a challenge for himself, to see if he could deviate from the complex narratives which he was known for and create something just as profound while comparatively straight-forward. The book follows Toru Watanabe during his college years. He is friends with and falls in love with a girl named Naoko. Naoko was the girlfriend of Toru's best friend, Kizuki, who committed suicide some years prior to their reunion once Toru began college. We follow Toru over the course of two years, as he goes through bursts of loneliness and constant alcoholism, and as Naoko slowly falls deeper and deeper down a hole filled with mental issues stemming from Kizuki's suicide, eventually culminating in her own suicide by the end of the book.


The heart of this book comes from the details surrounding Toru and Naoko's relationship. Behind Toru's love for Naoko is his grief for the death of Kizuki. Toru holds on to the memory of his friend through Naoko. His grief and trauma resulting from his suicide affects Toru's entire personality, or rather is lack thereof. Some criticize the book for this aspect, but upon reflection it feels like an intention part of the story. Kizuki's death marks the beginning of a life surrounded by death for Toru. Soaked in the tragedy of his death, Toru is emotionally distant and alienated from the people around him. Kizuki was Toru's only friend, and so once he looses this anchor to the rest of the world, Toru takes on this new, blank personality.


Similarly for Naoko, Kizuki's death slowly eats away at her mental health. In the last sentence of the first chapter, Toru makes the realization, many years after the events of the book, that Naoko never loved him. Arguably, throughout the book, Naoko is still in love with Naoko. This correlates with the escalation of her and Toru's relationship, as once they become more intimate, her mental health becomes far worse. Naoko remarks to Toru that he reminds her too much of Kizuki, and this is ultimately the reason for her worsening mental state. While Toru is trying to remain close to the memory of Kizuki by being with Naoko, Naoko cannot escape his memory, although apart of her doesn't want to, despite it being the only path for her recovery.


This dynamic is why Naoko decides to end her life in the finale of the book. The side plot of the book has Toru fall in love with another woman, Midori, which becomes an open fact between him and Naoko. Naoko is aware of the fact that Toru is resisting his urge to pursue his relationship with Midori out of an obligation towards her. Naoko therefore kills herself for two reasons. Firstly, to release Toru of his connected to her, and thus Kizuki, allowing him to grow as a person. Secondly, her suicide is a submission to her love for Kizuki, and so kills herself to join him in death. Arguably, contrary to Toru's realization, I interpret this as an act of love by Naoko. Why Toru sees it differently, I'm not sure. Another interpretation of her suicide I found was that Naoko was incapable of love, which explains why Kizuki commits suicide during their relationship. It is once she makes this observation about herself upon beginning her relationship with Toru that her mental health declines, eventually leading to her committing suicide out of guilt. While interesting, I don't think this is supported by the text, especially since it undermines the theme of accepting death as apart of life and being able to learn and grow from it. It's more likely that the part about Naoko mistreating Kizuki is real, which could add to her decision to end her life, as she doesn't want to make the same mistake with Toru, expressing her love for him.



As stated, this book is not without its flaws. As widely observed, the female characters of this book come off as shallow and/or incompetent. The character most blatantly guilty of this is Midori, Toru's second lover. Her only real character trait is being overtly horny, which isn't interesting or fun to read. She does have one great monologue, critiquing the leftist-marxist movement at their school for being insincere and only existing as a popularity contest for men to seduce young women who are too stupid to understand what they're talking about. Besides this one tidbit, Midori is a mostly static character that exists solely to add complexity to Toru and Naoko's relationship. I took notes following each chapter of the book while reading it, and the chapters involving Toru and Midori exclusively I had nothing or almost nothing of note to write down.


In the same vein, the other point this book is most often criticized for is being far too sexual. Kafka of the Shore did not stray away from sexuality either, so I had expected it when I started this book. However, while the sexual segments of Kafka felt meaningful and emotional, representing some deeper emotion going on within the characters, often times in Norwegian Wood felt either forced and unnecessary, or just a shallow imitation of the ones from Kafka. I should state the obvious, which is that sex is of course an important part of these stories, and I don't think that they should be removed by any means, especially when they can be so beautifully written thanks to Murakami. That being said, I think that Murakami forgets what makes sex so important and impactful in his other works in this book, so they often don't contribute much to the story other than to reduce or trivialize certain characters interactions.


Overall, I'd give Norwegian Wood an 8/10. I enjoyed it, but I'm not as excited to reread this one as I am Kafka on the Shore.
