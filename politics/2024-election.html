2024 Election: The Aftermath



This last week following the results of the 2024 presidential election has been defined both by a period self-reflection by Democrats and controversy by Republicans. Trump's presidency has hardly yet began, and even in this brief glimpse of what's to come, we have witnessed nothing but a variety of horrible decision regarding his cabinet and suggestions of new policies which may-or-may-not which promise to betray the people of this country. In any case, that's a topic for another time, once the dust has settled and we have a more concrete view of his administration.


What I actually want to focus on is the self-reflection. Understandably, many prominent figures within the Democratic party have expresses some amount of desire to "change" the party; to understand why we've failed to win and adjust our strategy to avoid future losses and, of course, better represent the American people. The debate has two fronts, the ideological debate and the demographic debate. In short, on one hand you have a conversation about what direction should the party move in, either left or right, and on the other hand you have the question of what group of people should the Democratic party be targeting. Both sides are contingent on the belief that change within the party is, in fact, the answer to our problems. And of course if you're a political party rather than an ideological one, that is naturally the answer. Your responsibility is not to represent any particular set of beliefs, but to represent the people and their beliefs.


I want to focus in on the necessity of change at all. Government, first and foremost, has the responsibility to improve the lives of the people which it governs, which it then has to balance with the political desire to keep people happy. Unfortunately, these goals do not always intersect each other. The awful reality of democracy is that the people don't always know (in some metaphysical sense, supposing such an answer exists) what is best for them. Not everyone has the ability to recognize and distinguish between what feels better, and what is actually better. This is what makes demagogues like Donald Trump so dangerous; Trump's ability to exploit the emotions of his misinformed political base, stoking fear and hate to advance his cause, makes him a rhetorically effective political leader among the casually political. That being said, when this is your opposition, how do you properly address defeat? Better yet, how do you combat the type of narrative pushed by Donald Trump without resorting to the same behavior?


I've found that there is a certain burden in arguing positions to Republicans. The difficult reality is that often the most "correct" opinion require the most work to reach, and usually when confronted with this dilemma most people choose the more intuitive and "natural" explanation, even if it means being wrong. It's easier to blame economic problems on immigrants than to read a dense and technical academic paper reviewing trade policies. Conservatives, naturally, thrive on simple yet incorrect explanations of the world. To progress society requires progressive thought, which requires effort and a particular creativity of thought that being social conservative definitionally lacks. Not that progressive thought is inherently more correct than conservative thought, just that it happens to be so more often than not. To return to politics, the big question for Democrats going forward will be how do you advocate for progressive policy without categorically rejecting the concerns of half the country? And can you maintain objectivity while doing so?


That being said, the question of what direction should the Democratic party move in following this election is complicated. You can't responsibility move to closer to the center without sacrificing the well-being of Americans, and you can't shift further to the left without losing more voters, forever dooming the country to Republican rule and thus again sacrificing the well-being of the country. So how do you target a population of people that is, to be blunt, less educated, while not outright ignoring them? Do you even have an obligation to do so?


Ideally, the solution is to take a more grassroots approach over the next four years and engage in a collective effort to educate the people of America. But more likely than not, this will turn out to be wishful thinking. People are suborn, careless, and especially as of late, anti-intellectual, traits which makes this task near impossible. We may have to live with the unfortunate reality that an entire generation of Americans have been permanently ruined by conservative thought. Perhaps we may not have anything to do, and Donald Trump will do all the work of shifting America back to the left while he's in office. It's entirely possible that we may not get the opportunity to elect more Democrats to office. There's a lot to fear about the next four year, both culturally and politically, but in the meantime, while we still have a democracy, I leave you with these questions to ponder.

I want to note this is not me critiquing democracy or suggesting anything philosophically about the nature of people or whatever, I am just expressing my frustration with the state of the country. Ideology happens manifests itself cancerously within our government, and this isn't a new phenomenon. People have always been a plague in opposition to efficiency, and this election is but further evidence of that. When presented with a better world, you will always have people who complain that it costed too much.

- Jackson
